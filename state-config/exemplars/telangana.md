# State Exemplar — Telangana (Rent Control)

## Applicable State Rent Control Act
- **Act:** Andhra Pradesh Buildings (Lease, Rent and Eviction) Control Act 1960 (A.P. Act No. 15 of 1960) — continues to apply to Telangana post the Andhra Pradesh Reorganisation Act 2014 (bifurcation effective 2 June 2014) by virtue of Section 101 of the 2014 Act
- **Scope:** Buildings in notified urban areas of Telangana (and Andhra Pradesh) let for residence, business or any other purpose

## Forum nomenclature
- **First instance:** `BEFORE THE RENT CONTROLLER, [PLACE]` (Principal Junior Civil Judge designated ex officio as Rent Controller)
- **Appellate (Hyderabad):** Chief Judge, City Small Causes Court, Hyderabad
- **Appellate (other districts):** Chief Rent Controller / District Judge
- **Revisional:** High Court for the State of Telangana — Article 227 of the Constitution + Section 22 of the 1960 Act (revisional jurisdiction)

## Section 106 TPA notice position
- **NOT MANDATORY.** The Andhra Pradesh Buildings (Lease, Rent and Eviction) Control Act 1960 is a self-contained code for eviction.
- Position in **V. Dhanapal Chettiar v. Yesodai Ammal, (1979) 4 SCC 214** directly applies — Section 106 TPA notice is not separately required for grounds under Section 10.
- However, a statutory demand notice under Section 10(2)(i) for default-of-rent ground is mandatory before initiating eviction proceedings.

## Standard rent / fair rent fixation methodology
- **Section 4** — Fair rent fixation by Rent Controller
- Formula: market value of site + cost of construction + reasonable return on capital + statutory permitted increases
- **Section 4(2)** — Factors to be considered: prevailing rates of rent for similar accommodation, locality, situation, taxes, etc.
- **Section 5-7** — Permitted increases for repairs, taxes, amenities

## Rent deposit procedure
- **Section 8** — Tenant may deposit rent with Rent Controller / Rent Court where landlord refuses to accept
- **Section 9** — Deposit operates as valid tender; defeats default-of-rent eviction ground if statutory conditions complied with
- Deposit must be regular and continuous to preserve defence

## Summary procedure (if any)
- NO Chapter IIIA DRC-equivalent strict summary procedure
- Section 10 proceedings before Rent Controller follow ordinary tribunal-style procedure with affidavit evidence permitted

## Pagdi / customary tenancy (if any)
- NOT statutorily recognized in Telangana / Andhra Pradesh

## Model Tenancy Act 2021 adoption status
- **Telangana:** **NOT YET ADOPTED.** Telangana continues with the 1960 AP Act; no MTA 2021 adoption notification by Telangana State Government as of current verification
- **Andhra Pradesh (sister State, for comparison):** Andhra Pradesh was reported as one of the FIRST STATES to take steps toward adopting Model Tenancy Act 2021 — verify current notification status with AP Housing Department before drafting in AP

## State-specific notes
- **Continuing applicability of the 1960 AP Act** — post the Andhra Pradesh Reorganisation Act 2014, the AP Buildings (Lease, Rent and Eviction) Control Act 1960 continues to apply to both Telangana and Andhra Pradesh until either successor State enacts its own replacement legislation
- **Hyderabad-specific forum** — Chief Judge of the City Small Causes Court Hyderabad exercises appellate jurisdiction over Hyderabad rent matters; the City Small Causes Court is a specialised forum for Hyderabad
- **Section 10(3) comparative hardship test** — mandatory consideration in bona-fide requirement applications; Rent Controller must weigh hardship to landlord against hardship to tenant
- **Section 32 exemption** — Government may exempt certain categories of buildings from the Act's provisions; always verify exemption notifications before filing
- **₹3,500 monthly rent threshold** — premises with monthly rent above this threshold may fall outside certain protective provisions; verify with current notification
- **Section 15 execution** — Rent Controller has powers of a Civil Court for execution of eviction orders
- Pre-2014 jurisprudence under the AP Act remains binding in Telangana under saving provisions; Telangana High Court (constituted in 2019) inherits the jurisprudence developed by the erstwhile common High Court for AP and Telangana
